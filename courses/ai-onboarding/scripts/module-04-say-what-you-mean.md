## THE FOUR ELEMENTS

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
Now let's pull back and look at what makes a prompt work structurally. Not as a formulaâ€”as a way of seeing.

*[VISUAL: Four elements appear, building one at a time. Simple. Clean. No jargon.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
Every effective prompt shares four elements. Not always in the same order. Not always in the same proportion. But they're always there.

**Element one: Context.**

*[VISUAL: The word CONTEXT appears.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
Who is this for? What's the situation? What has already happened? This is your tacit knowledge, made explicit. It's the stuff your brain knows automatically that the machine needs you to say out loud.

Joe telling the machine about the trade show, about Lisa's role, about the feature she likedâ€”that's context. Without it, the machine is guessing. With it, the machine is working from the same reality you are.

**Element two: Task.**

*[VISUAL: The word TASK appears.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
What specifically do you need produced? Not "an email"â€”that's a format. A task is: write a follow-up email that suggests a 30-minute demo next Tuesday or Wednesday. The more precisely you define the task, the less the machine has to improvise. And machine improvisation is where generic output comes from.

**Element three: Tone.**

*[VISUAL: The word TONE appears.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
How should this sound? And remember from Module 3â€”"professional" is not a tone. "Short, warm, not salesy" is a tone. "Factual and solutions-oriented" is a tone. "Like I'm talking to a colleague I respect but don't know well" is a tone. The more specific you are about how something should *feel*, the closer the output matches your voice.

**Element four: Boundaries.**

*[VISUAL: The word BOUNDARIES appears.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
What should this *not* be? This is the element most people skip, and it's one of the most powerful. "Don't oversell." "Keep it under 200 words." "Don't use jargon." "Skip the preamble." Telling the machine what to avoid is often more useful than telling it what to includeâ€”because it eliminates the most common failure modes before they happen.

*[Beat.]*

*[VISUAL: All four elements visible together: CONTEXT â€” TASK â€” TONE â€” BOUNDARIES]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
Context, Task, Tone, Boundaries. That's it. Not a template to fill in. A mental checklist to make sure you've said what you mean before you hit enter.

And noticeâ€”these aren't AI concepts. These are communication concepts. If you were delegating this email to a new hire, you'd give them the same four things. The machine is no different. It needs what any capable stranger would need to do good work on your behalf.

## THE COMMON MISTAKES

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
Now. Before we move on, let's name the three most common mistakes people make even when they're *trying* to write good prompts. Because knowing the framework doesn't make you immune to shortcuts.

*[VISUAL: Three mistakes, appearing one at a time.]*

**Mistake one: The adjective trap.**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
We covered this with Joe, but it's worth repeating because it's the most persistent habit. Adding "detailed," "comprehensive," "thorough," "well-researched," or "engaging" to a prompt does almost nothing. These are wishes, not instructions. "Write a detailed report" and "write a report" will produce nearly identical output. What changes the output is *what you put in the report,* not what adjectives you attach to it.

*[Beat.]*

**Mistake two: The missing audience.**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
WHO is going to read this? Most people never say. But a project update for your director reads completely differently than the same update for your team. A proposal for a CFO leads with numbers. The same proposal for a COO leads with operations impact. If you don't specify the audience, the machine writes for no one in particularâ€”and text written for no one in particular is useful to no one in particular.

*[Beat.]*

**Mistake three: No definition of done.**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**  
How will you know when this is right? What does "good" look like? If your only evaluation criterion is "I'll know it when I see it," you're going to burn time iterating in circles. Before you prompt, decide: What would make me hit send immediately? What would make me rewrite the whole thing? The clearer your finish line, the fewer laps you run.

## NOTES FOR PRODUCTION

**Part Two opening:**
- Energy shift from Part One. Part One was reflective, philosophical, building understanding. Part Two should feel like rolling up your sleeves. Same narrator voice, but the tone says: now we work.
- Keep the Part Two intro under 30 seconds. The audience already bought inâ€”don't re-sell them.

**Cold open montage:**
- Quick cuts. Four or five people, diverse settings, all making the same mistake. Under 45 seconds total. The point isn't to dwellâ€”it's to show this is universal, not just Joe.
- These characters don't need names or backstories. They're quick flashes of recognition.
