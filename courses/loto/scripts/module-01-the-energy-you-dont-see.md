## THE DIFFERENCE

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Here's what most people think lockout/tagout is: you put a padlock on a breaker before you work on a machine. Simple procedure. Common sense. The kind of thing you learn on day one and never really think about again.

*[VISUAL: A padlock on a breaker. The simplest possible image of LOTO.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
And that's not wrong, exactly. But it's so incomplete that it might as well be.

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Single VISUAL, not a PANEL sequence, because this is a standalone reference image — it illustrates a point, not a narrative moment. The script-integration guide says use VISUAL for standalone images, PANEL for sequences. -->

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Lockout/tagout isn't a procedure. It's a barrier. The only barrier between you and the energy stored in the machine you're about to put your hands inside.

And the first step to respecting that barrier is understanding what's on the other side of it.

## FIVE TYPES

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
When someone says "hazardous energy," most people think electricity. That's fair. Electricity is the obvious one — the one that gets all the safety posters.

*[VISUAL: A simple electrical hazard symbol.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
But OSHA's Control of Hazardous Energy standard — that's the regulation behind lockout/tagout — recognizes five types. And if you only think about one of them, the other four can kill you while you're busy being careful about electricity.

*[TRANSITION: subject-to-subject]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Subject-to-subject transition as we move between the five energy types. Each one is a different "subject" in the same thematic space (hazardous energy). The learner connects them as a family of dangers, not a list. -->

*[PANEL: Close-up on an open electrical panel. Breakers visible. Color-coded wires. The hum is almost visible in the image — sharp, precise, technical.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
**Electrical energy.** The one you know. Current flowing through conductors. The breaker panel, the junction box, the motor leads. Touch the wrong thing and current flows through you instead of the wire. This is the danger everyone respects.

But here's the thing about electricity that people forget: even after you cut the power, some components store charge. Capacitors. They're in motors, control boards, switch gears. They hold electrical energy the way a battery does, and they can discharge it through you in a fraction of a second.

Flipping the breaker isn't enough. You have to deal with what's still inside.

*[PANEL: Close-up on a hydraulic cylinder. A pressure gauge reads 2000 PSI. The line connects to a massive actuator — the kind that moves heavy things with terrifying precision.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
**Hydraulic and pneumatic energy.** Pressurized fluid — oil in a hydraulic system, air in a pneumatic one. These systems do the heavy lifting in manufacturing. They press, they clamp, they lift, they cut.

Two thousand PSI in a hydraulic line. That's over a hundred times the pressure in your car tires. Close the valve and that pressure doesn't vanish. It's still in the line, still in the cylinder, still waiting. A fitting fails, a connection slips, and that pressurized fluid finds a new exit. At that pressure, a pinhole leak can pierce skin.

*[PANEL: A coiled spring mechanism visible inside an open machine guard. The spring is compressed. Tight. The metal strains against its restraint.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
**Mechanical energy.** Springs. Flywheels. Anything that stores energy in tension, compression, or rotation. A compressed spring doesn't care whether the machine is on or off. It's loaded. It wants to release. And if you're between the spring and where it wants to go when it lets go, the machine being "off" doesn't help you.

*[Beat.]*

*[PANEL: A heavy steel press platen suspended above a work surface. The hydraulic cylinder holding it up is visible. Between the platen and the table — a gap where a person works. Where a person reaches.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Single panel, high detail on the gap. McCloud's panel density: one large panel here forces the viewer to sit with this image. The suspended weight. The gap. The human-sized space where someone would be. The visual does the work — the narration adds the physics. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
**Gravitational energy.** Anything suspended. Anything raised. A press platen held up by hydraulic pressure. A crane load held by a brake. A platform raised on a scissor lift. Gravity is constant and patient. The moment the thing holding that weight fails, everything falls. And gravity doesn't give warnings.

*[PANEL: Chemical supply lines — color-coded pipes entering a machine. Valves. Warning labels. The quiet infrastructure of reactive substances flowing through a system.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
**Chemical energy.** Reactive substances in supply lines. Corrosives. Solvents. Gases. Even something as ordinary as steam. These systems feed the process, and when you open a machine to work on it, those supply lines may still be live. A residual chemical in a line you assumed was empty. A valve that didn't fully close. A reaction that starts when two substances meet inside the machine you just opened.

*[Beat.]*

## OFF ISN'T SAFE

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
And here's where the real problem begins.

*[PANEL: A conveyor belt. Still. Not moving. It looks safe. It looks off. It looks like a machine you could walk up to and start working on.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
What does a machine look like when it's off?

*[Beat.]*

It looks like this. Quiet. Still. Nothing moving. The controls are in the off position. To every sense you have, this machine is safe.

*[PANEL: subject-to-subject — The same conveyor. Same stillness. But now the frame includes the hydraulic cylinder underneath, gauge still showing pressure. The spring mechanism visible through an open access point, still compressed. The electrical panel with no lock.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Subject-to-subject transition. Same machine, but now we see what was always there. The first panel showed what the worker sees. The second shows what's actually true. The juxtaposition IS the argument — McCloud: subject-to-subject forces the viewer to build the relationship between "looks safe" and "isn't safe." -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
But "off" and "safe" are not the same word.

*[Beat.]*

"Off" means the controls are in the off position. "Safe" means every energy source has been isolated, every stored energy hazard has been addressed, and the isolation has been verified.

"Off" is a button you push. "Safe" is a process you complete.

*[Beat.]*

The machine in the cold open was "off." The stop button worked. The belt wasn't moving. To the worker's eyes, it was perfectly safe.

But the electrical disconnect wasn't locked. Anyone could flip it. The hydraulic lines were still pressurized. The worker hadn't checked for stored energy because, as far as he could tell, the machine was off.

It was off. It wasn't safe.

## STORED ENERGY

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
This is the concept that matters most in this entire course, and it's the one most people skip past.

**Stored energy** is the energy that remains in a system after you've isolated it from its source.

*[PANEL: moment-to-moment — A hand turns a valve to the closed position.]*

*[PANEL: moment-to-moment — The same hand reaches for the pressure gauge on the line. The gauge still reads pressure.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Moment-to-moment. McCloud: this transition slows time down, making the viewer feel each micro-step. I'm using it here because this is the exact moment where complacency kills — the worker closes the valve and assumes the job is done. The slow pacing forces the learner to notice: the valve is closed, but the pressure is still there. Each panel is a beat, and the beat between them is where the learning happens. -->

*[GUTTER: The learner should feel the gap between "I closed the valve" and "the line is still pressurized." The action felt complete. The hazard isn't.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
You closed the valve. Good. New energy can't enter the line.

But the energy that was already in the line? It's still there. The fluid is still pressurized. The spring is still compressed. The capacitor is still charged. The load is still suspended.

Isolation stops new energy from entering. It doesn't remove the energy that's already present.

*[Beat.]*

That's why lockout/tagout has six steps, not three. The first three get you to isolation. The next three deal with what's left.

## THE SIX STEPS — PREVIEW

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
We'll spend all of Module 2 on the six-step lockout sequence. For now, just understand why it exists.

*[VISUAL: The six steps listed simply — Prepare, Shutdown, Isolate, Lock, Stored Energy, Verify.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Prepare. Shut down. Isolate. Lock. Address stored energy. Verify.

Most people, if they do anything at all, jump straight to step four. Slap a lock on the breaker. Feel safe.

But without step one, they don't know all the energy sources. Without step two, they may not have stopped the machine properly. Without step three, they've only locked one source out of five. Without step five, the energy already in the system is still there. Without step six, they have no confirmation that any of it worked.

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
The procedure is simple. Six steps. A padlock. A tag.

And that simplicity is exactly what makes it dangerous. Because simple feels like something you can shortcut. Something you already know. Something you don't need to be careful about this time because it's just a quick fix, you'll be done in five minutes, nobody needs to know.

*[Beat.]*

The moment you treat a six-step procedure like it's beneath your attention is the moment it fails to protect you.

- A) The machine is safe to work on

## PRODUCTION NOTES

### Panel-Logic Decisions

**Cold open panel sequence (6 panels):**
- 3 aspect-to-aspect establishing panels → 3 action-to-action narrative panels → 1 subject-to-subject reveal → implication gutter
- Reasoning: Classic cold open pattern from the sequential art methodology. Atmosphere first (the learner enters the space), then action (the worker does the reasonable thing), then the subject-to-subject cut reveals what the worker doesn't see. The implication gutter carries the weight of the consequence without depicting it.

**Five energy types (5 panels):**
- Each energy type gets one high-detail panel. McCloud: these are picture-specific combinations — the image IS the subject, the narration adds context. High detail because the learner needs to recognize these specific things in their workplace.

**Convergence diagram:**
- Single VISUAL, not a panel. Tufte-style technical illustration. Five energy sources converging on the human work space. This is the module's visual thesis.

**"Off isn't safe" sequence (2 panels):**
- Subject-to-subject. Same machine, different information visible. The transition itself is the argument.

**Stored energy moment-to-moment (2 panels):**
- Hand closes valve → gauge still reads pressure. Moment-to-moment slows time to force the learner to notice the gap between action and safety. This is the most important micro-sequence in the module.

**Callback (3 panels):**
- Same environment, different choices. The lockout station moves from background detail to foreground action. The verification panel (pressing start, nothing happens) is the emotional resolution.

### Transition Pattern
```
aspect-to-aspect → action-to-action → subject-to-subject (cold open)
scene-to-scene (to teaching)
subject-to-subject (five energy types, within section)
subject-to-subject ("off isn't safe")
moment-to-moment (stored energy)
scene-to-scene (callback)
action-to-action → subject-to-subject (resolution)
```

### Abstraction Map
- Cold open: Medium detail (real environment, faceless worker)
- Energy type panels: High detail (equipment the learner must recognize)
- "Off isn't safe": Medium detail (same machine, new information)
- Stored energy: High detail, close-up (the gauge, the hand, the valve)
- Thesis moments ("off and safe are not the same word"): Typography-heavy, minimal image
- Callback: Medium detail echoing cold open

### Gutter Inventory
1. **Cold open — unlocked disconnect:** Learner imagines someone flipping the breaker while the worker is inside the machine
2. **Cold open — second person approaching:** Learner imagines the consequence. Implication gutter. Never shown.
3. **Stored energy — valve closed, gauge reads pressure:** Learner feels the gap between "I did the thing" and "the hazard persists"
4. **Callback — full sequence compressed:** Learner sees the complete barrier in place. Relief. Resolution.

### Tone Calibration
- Cold open: Quiet tension. Not dramatic. Just... wrong. The wrongness should feel ordinary, because it is.
- Five energy types: Authoritative but not lecturing. The narrator knows these systems. Specific details (2000 PSI, pinhole leaks) build credibility with the audience.
- "Off isn't safe": This is the thesis. Direct. No hedging. The two short definitions ("Off is a button. Safe is a process.") should land with weight.
- Stored energy: Slightly urgent. This is where people die — not from the obvious danger but from the leftover.
- Callback: Warm. Resolution. The same scene, done right. The weight of a padlock as protection. The closing line ("everyone goes home safe") earns its simplicity.

### Character Notes
- The worker is never named. Referred to as "he" only in the cold open narration, then shifts to "you" for teaching. The facelessness is deliberate — McCloud's abstraction principle. Hard hat, safety glasses, work gloves obscure identity. The audience should see their own hands, not a character's.
- No face is ever shown. Not in the cold open, not in the callback. The closest we get is the edge of a hard hat above gloved hands.
