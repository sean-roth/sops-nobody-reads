## WHY ORDER MATTERS

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Module 1 introduced the six steps. Now we're going to walk through each one. Not because the steps are hard to understand — they're not. But because understanding *why* each step exists, and *why* they go in this order, is the difference between following a checklist and actually being safe.

*[VISUAL: The six steps — Prepare, Shutdown, Isolate, Lock, Stored Energy, Verify — arranged vertically, each one building on the one before it.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Think of these as layers. Each step handles something the previous step couldn't. Skip a layer and there's a gap. A gap in this process is a gap in your protection.

---

## STEP ONE: PREPARE FOR SHUTDOWN

*[PANEL: A worker at a desk or workbench, reviewing a machine-specific lockout procedure form. The form has columns: energy source, location, lockout device needed. It's filled out. Specific.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Single panel, high detail on the form itself. Picture-specific combination — the image carries the teaching weight. The form is the subject. The narration explains why it matters. High detail because this is a specific artifact the learner needs to recognize and use. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Before you touch anything, you need to know what you're dealing with.

Step one is homework. Find the machine-specific lockout procedure. Every machine with hazardous energy should have one — a form that lists every energy source, where it enters the system, and what device you need to isolate it.

*[Beat.]*

This is the step that most people skip. It feels like paperwork. But this form is the map. Without it, you're guessing which energy sources are present, guessing where to isolate them, guessing what stored energy might remain.

Guessing gets people hurt.

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
The other part of preparation: notify. Every person whose work is affected by the lockout needs to know.

What is being locked out. Why. How long. Who's responsible. Who to contact.

*[Beat.]*

This isn't courtesy. This is protection. An affected employee who doesn't know about the lockout is an affected employee who might try to start the machine.

## STEP TWO: EQUIPMENT SHUTDOWN

*[PANEL: action-to-action — Hands on a control panel. The operator pushes the stop button. The machine responds — a conveyor belt slows, rollers decelerate, movement ceases.]*

*[PANEL: moment-to-moment — The same machine. Almost still. A flywheel is still turning, barely. Coasting to a stop. Not stopped yet.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Action-to-action for the stop button press, then moment-to-moment for the coast-down. McCloud: moment-to-moment slows time. The flywheel still turning is the point — the machine is stopping but hasn't stopped. Most people see "slowing" and think "done." This panel forces the learner to see the gap between decelerating and fully stopped. The patience of this moment matters. -->

*[GUTTER: The learner should feel the difference between "slowing down" and "stopped." The flywheel is still carrying energy. It looks almost done. Almost isn't done.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Step two: shut the machine down using normal stopping procedures.

This sounds obvious. It mostly is. But there are two things people get wrong here.

First: verify that all moving parts have actually stopped. Not "almost stopped." Not "slowing down." Stopped. Flywheels coast. Gears have momentum. Spindles decelerate. Wait for zero.

Second: use the normal stopping procedure. The one the manufacturer documented. The one your employer trained you on. Don't improvise. Some machines have specific shutdown sequences — steps that need to happen in order to avoid damaging the equipment or creating new hazards. Pulling the plug on a machine that needs a controlled shutdown can be its own kind of dangerous.

---

## STEP THREE: ENERGY ISOLATION

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Step three: disconnect the machine from every energy source.

Every source. Not just the electrical.

*[PANEL: action-to-action — A hand switches an electrical disconnect to the OFF position. We can see the breaker connections physically separate. Clear, definitive.]*

*[PANEL: action-to-action — A different hand turns a valve to CLOSED on a hydraulic supply line. The valve handle perpendicular to the pipe.]*

*[PANEL: action-to-action — Chemical supply lines. A valve closed, and a cap placed on the end of a disconnected line.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Three action-to-action panels in sequence. Each energy source gets its own isolation panel. This is procedural pacing — the repetition of the same transition type (action-to-action) creates the feeling of a checklist being worked through. Each panel shows a DIFFERENT type of energy being addressed. The variety of subjects within the consistent transition type teaches: same procedure, applied to each source independently. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Isolation means different things for different energy types. For electrical: the disconnect switch goes to OFF and you visually verify the breaker connections are open. For hydraulic and pneumatic: valves go to CLOSED. For chemical supply lines: valves close, and where possible, lines are bled or capped.

The key word is "isolate." Not "reduce." Not "minimize." Isolate. The energy source and the machine are no longer connected. Period.

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Your machine-specific procedure — the one from step one — tells you exactly which isolation points exist and where they are. This is why step one isn't optional. You can't isolate what you haven't identified.

## STEP FOUR: APPLY LOCKOUT/TAGOUT DEVICES

*[PANEL: moment-to-moment — A hand slides a lock through the hasp on the electrical disconnect. The lock clicks shut.]*

*[PANEL: moment-to-moment — The same hand attaches a tag. Close-up on the tag as it's filled out — name, date, reason.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Moment-to-moment for the lock and tag. McCloud: slowing time here gives this act the weight it deserves. This is the physical act that creates the barrier. The click of the lock should feel significant. The tag being filled out should feel deliberate. These aren't fast actions — they're intentional ones. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Step four is the one everyone recognizes. The padlock. The tag.

This is the physical barrier. Once this lock is on, the disconnect cannot be moved to the ON position. Not by accident. Not by someone who didn't see a sign. Not by a remote-control system. Not by anyone, for any reason, unless they have the key.

*[Beat.]*

And only one person has the key. You.

*[VISUAL: A lockout hasp with three padlocks on it. Three different colors. Each one belongs to a different worker.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Here's the principle that makes the whole system work: **one person, one lock.**

Each worker applies their own lock. Each lock has one key. No master keys. No shared locks. No skeleton keys that maintenance supervisors carry "just in case."

*[Beat.]*

If three people are working on the same machine, three locks go on the isolation point. The machine cannot be re-energized until all three people have removed their own lock. No one removes someone else's lock. Ever.

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Think about what this means. Your lock is your personal guarantee that the machine will not start while your hands are inside it. Not your supervisor's guarantee. Not the company's guarantee. Yours. You put it on. You take it off. Only when you are done and clear.

*[Beat.]*

This is why master keys are prohibited. A master key means someone else can override your decision that this machine isn't safe to start yet. A master key means your protection depends on someone else's judgment about whether you're done.

Your life, someone else's judgment. That's not a system. That's a gamble.

---

## TAGS ARE NOT LOCKS

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
A word about tags.

*[PANEL: subject-to-subject — A lock on a disconnect. Solid. Metal. Cannot be removed without the key. Next to it: a tag hanging on a wire. Paper. Can be removed by anyone with fingers and a moment of inattention.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Subject-to-subject. The two objects side by side force the comparison. McCloud: the viewer builds the relationship between the two subjects. The lock is a physical barrier. The tag is a communication device. Seeing them together makes the difference visceral. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
A tag is a communication device. It tells people that a lockout is in progress. It identifies who applied it and why.

A tag is not a barrier. You cannot physically prevent a machine from starting with a tag. You can only ask.

*[Beat.]*

OSHA's standard is clear: lockout is the default. Tagout alone is only acceptable when the machine physically cannot accommodate a lock — and even then, the employer must prove that the tagout program provides equivalent protection. Tags supplement locks. They don't replace them.

The cold open showed you what a request looks like: a sign on a breaker panel. Someone walked right past it. A tag without a lock is the same vulnerability with a fancier label.

## STEP FIVE: CHECK FOR STORED ENERGY

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
This is the step that separates people who understand lockout from people who just follow a checklist.

*[PANEL: action-to-action — A hand opens a bleed valve on a hydraulic line. Fluid drains into a catch pan. The pressure gauge needle drops from 2000 to zero.]*

*[PANEL: action-to-action — A safety block being inserted under a raised press platen. The block is solid steel. It sits between the platen and the table — the space where a person works.]*

*[PANEL: action-to-action — A spring mechanism being carefully released. The spring decompresses in a controlled way. Tension leaving the system.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Three action-to-action panels. Each shows a DIFFERENT stored energy type being addressed — hydraulic, gravitational, mechanical. Same transition type, different subjects. The repetition pattern matches step three (isolation) deliberately. The learner should feel the parallel: step three isolates the sources, step five addresses what's left. Same discipline, applied to a different problem. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
You've isolated the energy sources. New energy can't enter the system. But the energy already inside hasn't gone anywhere.

Hydraulic lines are still pressurized. Bleed them through the relief valves until the gauges read zero.

Suspended loads are still elevated. Lower them if possible, or block them so they physically cannot fall.

Compressed springs are still loaded. Release them carefully, or block the parts they could move.

Capacitors are still charged. Discharge them per the manufacturer's procedure.

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Each of these is specific to the machine and the energy type. This is why your machine-specific procedure lists stored energy hazards and how to address each one. Generic procedures can't tell you where the bleed valve is on your hydraulic press. Only the procedure for your specific machine can do that.

---

## STEP SIX: VERIFY ISOLATION

*[PANEL: moment-to-moment — A hand on the machine's start button. The worker is about to press it. The machine is locked out, bled down, blocked, tagged.]*

*[PANEL: moment-to-moment — The finger presses the button. Nothing happens. The machine doesn't respond.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Moment-to-moment for the verification. This is the most important single action in the entire procedure, and it's the one most people skip. McCloud: moment-to-moment slows time to a crawl. The viewer feels each micro-second of the finger pressing the button. The stillness that follows — the machine NOT responding — is the payoff. The absence of action is the proof. I want the learner to feel the relief of that silence. -->

*[GUTTER: The learner should feel the beat between pressing the button and nothing happening. That silence is the sound of safety. That's the confirmation. That's the proof.]*

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Step six. The one that proves everything else worked.

Try to start the machine. Press the operating controls. Attempt to energize it.

If nothing happens, the lockout is complete. The machine is safe.

*[Beat.]*

If something DOES happen — if the machine responds, if a light comes on, if something moves — you missed something. Stop. Go back. Re-isolate. Find the energy source you didn't identify.

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Verification isn't trust. It's proof. You don't trust that you got all the energy sources. You test it. You push the button and you wait for the silence.

*[Beat.]*

After verification, return all controls to the OFF or NEUTRAL position. Then inform the people in the area that you're about to begin work.

Now — and only now — it's safe to put your hands inside that machine.

## THE PSYCHOLOGY OF SKIPPING

*[TRANSITION: scene-to-scene]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
You now know the six steps. Prepare, shutdown, isolate, lock, stored energy, verify.

They aren't complicated. None of them are hard. Any single step takes seconds to minutes.

So why do people skip them?

*[Beat.]*

*[VISUAL: A short list, appearing as the narrator speaks — not as bullet points but as plain text, each line landing with weight.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Because the job is quick. Five-minute fix. In and out.

Because they've done it before. A hundred times. Nothing happened.

Because the machine is already off. They pressed the stop button. It's not moving.

Because no one's watching. It's end of shift. They just want to finish and go home.

Because the last guy didn't lock out either, and he's fine.

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
These are all real reasons. None of them are irrational. They come from the same place every shortcut comes from — the gap between what *usually* happens and what *can* happen.

Most of the time, nothing bad happens when you skip the steps. That's the truth. You do a quick fix without locking out and you're fine.

But "most of the time" is not "always." And the one time it isn't fine, there's no partial outcome. There's no minor version of getting electrocuted, or having a hydraulic press close on your arm, or having a spring mechanism release into your face.

*[Beat.]*

The procedure is simple. That's the whole point. It's simple enough that there is no good reason not to do it. Every time. Even the five-minute jobs. Especially the five-minute jobs.

## PRODUCTION NOTES

### Panel-Logic Decisions

**Cold open (2 panels + gutter):**
- Aspect-to-aspect establishing shot (sign on breaker) → action-to-action (hand flips breaker past the sign) → implication gutter
- Reasoning: The cold open is compressed compared to Module 1. We don't need to build the environment again — the learner has been here. What we need is the single image that crystallizes the module's thesis: a sign is not a lock. The peeling tape is the visual metaphor — protection that's already failing before someone even tests it.

**Six-step walk-through:**
- Each step gets its own section with panels matched to the pedagogical need:
  - Step 1 (Prepare): Single high-detail panel of the form. Picture-specific.
  - Step 2 (Shutdown): Action-to-action → moment-to-moment. The coast-down.
  - Step 3 (Isolate): Three action-to-action panels in sequence. Repetition pattern = checklist.
  - Step 4 (Lock): Moment-to-moment for the lock click. Weight and intention.
  - Step 5 (Stored energy): Three action-to-action panels matching step 3's pattern.
  - Step 6 (Verify): Moment-to-moment. Finger on button. Silence.

**One-person-one-lock:**
- Single VISUAL of three locks on one hasp. The image does the teaching.

**Tags vs locks:**
- Subject-to-subject. Lock and tag side by side. The comparison IS the argument.

**Psychology of skipping:**
- No panels. Typography carries this section. Plain text, each line landing as its own beat. McCloud's insight applied in reverse: by withholding images here, we force the learner to sit with the words. The absence of visuals after a visually rich module creates its own emphasis.

**Callback (3 panels):**
- Same breaker panel, lock replaces sign. Then inside the machine — calm, steady work. The emotional contrast between the cold open's tension and the callback's calm is the argument for the procedure.


### Gutter Inventory
1. **Cold open — hand flips breaker past sign:** Learner imagines Mike inside the machine when the power comes on
2. **Step 2 — flywheel coasting:** Learner feels the difference between slowing and stopped
3. **Step 6 — finger presses button, nothing happens:** Learner feels the relief of silence — proof the procedure worked
4. **Callback — sign replaced by lock:** Learner contrasts the vulnerability of the cold open with the security of the resolution

### Character Notes
- "Mike" is named in the cold open only through his sign. He's never seen. He represents every worker who depends on someone else's attention for their safety.
- The worker in the step-by-step walk-through is the same faceless, abstracted figure from Module 1. Continuity through anonymity.
- The hand that flips the breaker in the cold open is also faceless. The person isn't malicious — they just didn't see the sign, or didn't think it applied. The casualness of the action is the horror.
