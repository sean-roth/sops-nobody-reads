## RESTORING EQUIPMENT

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Before we get to the edge cases, let's close the loop on the basic procedure. You've locked the machine out. You've done the work. Now it's time to bring it back.

This has its own sequence, and it matters as much as the lockout itself.

*[VISUAL: Seven steps listed — Inspect, Clean Up, Replace Guards, Check Controls, Check for Personnel, Remove Locks, Re-energize.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Seven steps. Inspect the work — make sure it's actually finished and all your tools are accounted for. Clean up — parts, rags, work aids back where they belong. Replace guards — every guard goes back before the machine starts, with rare exceptions for startup adjustments. Check controls — everything in neutral or its safest position.

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Then the critical ones.

**Check for personnel.** Look at the work area. Every person who was working on or near this equipment must be clear. Accounted for. Notified that the lockout is being removed.

This is not a glance. This is a headcount. If three people were working on this machine, three people must be verified clear before any lock comes off.

**Remove your lock.** Your lock. Not someone else's. Only yours. The person who put the lock on takes the lock off. If your coworker went home and left their lock on, that's not your problem to solve — that's a procedure we'll cover in a moment.

**Re-energize.** Follow the manufacturer's startup procedures. Not the reverse of shutdown — the specific startup sequence documented for this equipment.

*[Beat.]*

The order matters here too. You don't remove locks before checking for personnel. You don't re-energize before replacing guards. Each step protects against a specific failure.

## GROUP LOCKOUT

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Most of the time, lockout is straightforward. One worker, one machine, one lock.

But maintenance doesn't always work that way.

*[PANEL: A piece of equipment with a lockout hasp. Five padlocks on it. Five different colors. A small group of workers around the machine, each in the process of their own task.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Single panel, high detail on the hasp with multiple locks. Picture-specific — the image teaches. Five locks on one hasp is visually striking and immediately communicates the principle: each person has their own protection. The number of locks = the number of people whose lives depend on this machine staying off. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
When multiple workers need to service the same equipment, each one applies their own lock. The principle doesn't change — one person, one lock. It scales.

But coordination matters. Someone needs to be in charge. A group coordinator — one authorized employee who manages the overall lockout. They don't replace the individual locks. They organize the process.

The coordinator reviews the lockout procedure with everyone before work begins. Each worker applies their lock. Each worker removes their lock when their part is done. The machine cannot restart until the last lock is off.

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
But here's where it gets interesting. What happens when the machine only has room for one lock?

*[PANEL: action-to-action — A single lock placed on a disconnect by the group coordinator.]*

*[PANEL: action-to-action — The coordinator takes the key to that lock and places it inside a small lockbox or cabinet.]*

*[PANEL: action-to-action — Five workers, each placing their own padlock on the lockbox. The key to the disconnect is now locked inside a box that requires five locks to be removed before it can be opened.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Three action-to-action panels in sequence. This is a procedural demonstration — cause and effect, step by step. The lockbox method is conceptually clever and the three-panel sequence lets the learner follow the logic: one lock on the machine, key in the box, everyone locks the box. By the third panel, the learner should have the "aha" — the machine can't start until every person has removed their lock from the box, which is the same protection as having every lock directly on the disconnect. McCloud: action-to-action is the clearest way to show a procedure being built. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
The lockbox method. One lock on the machine. The key to that lock goes in a lockbox. Every worker puts their lock on the lockbox.

The machine can't be re-energized until the lockbox is opened. The lockbox can't be opened until every worker removes their lock. Same protection. Different geometry.

*[Beat.]*

It's an elegant solution. And it exists because the principle — one person, one lock, every person has personal control over their own safety — doesn't bend. The physical setup adapts. The principle doesn't.

## SHIFT CHANGES

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Back to the cold open.

*[PANEL: The same disconnect from the cold open. Empty. No lock. The gap.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Callback panel to the cold open. Single image. The empty disconnect. McCloud: returning to an earlier image with established emotional weight. The learner already knows what this empty hasp means — they felt it in the cold open. Now we explain how to prevent it. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
The problem is simple: if the outgoing crew removes their locks before the incoming crew applies theirs, there's a window where the machine is unprotected.

The solution is equally simple: **overlap.**

*[PANEL: moment-to-moment — An incoming worker places their lock on the hasp. The outgoing worker's lock is still on it. Two locks. Both present.]*

*[PANEL: moment-to-moment — Now the outgoing worker removes their lock. The incoming worker's lock remains. The machine was never unprotected.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Moment-to-moment. Two panels. McCloud: moment-to-moment forces the viewer to see each micro-step. The critical insight is the ORDER — new lock ON before old lock OFF. The two panels make the overlap visible. At no point in this sequence is the hasp empty. That's the whole point, and the moment-to-moment pacing makes it impossible to miss. -->

*[GUTTER: The learner should feel the difference between this — seamless, overlapping, always protected — and the cold open, where the gap existed because the order was wrong.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
New lock on before old lock off. The incoming worker places their lock while the outgoing worker's lock is still in place. Then — and only then — the outgoing worker removes theirs.

At no point is the hasp empty. At no point is the machine unprotected. The transition is seamless because the locks overlap.

*[Beat.]*

Four minutes. That's what the cold open's gap was. And this is how you close it. Not with trust. Not with communication. With a padlock that's already there when the other one leaves.

## EMERGENCY LOCK REMOVAL

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Here's the scenario nobody wants to think about.

*[PANEL: A disconnect with a single lock on it. The shift is over. The plant is quiet. And the person whose lock this is? They're gone. Not in the building. Not answering their phone.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Single panel. The lock, the empty space around it. Aspect-to-aspect qualities — the stillness, the quiet, the "after hours" feeling. But the lock is the subject, and it's the problem. The learner should feel the tension: the machine needs to restart, but someone's lock is still on it, and that someone isn't here. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
A worker left for the day. Maybe they forgot their lock was still on. Maybe they had an emergency. Whatever the reason — their lock is on the disconnect, and they're not here to remove it.

The temptation is obvious: cut the lock. Start the machine. Deal with the paperwork later.

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
The procedure is specific and it exists to prevent exactly the disaster you're imagining.

First: verify that the authorized employee who applied the lock is not in the facility. Not in the building. Not in a different area. Not on break. Verified absent.

Second: make reasonable efforts to contact them. Call them. If you can't reach them, document the attempts.

Third — and this is the one people forget: the employee must be informed that their lock was removed *before* they return to work. Before they walk back into this facility. Before they approach any equipment.

*[Beat.]*

*[PANEL: subject-to-subject — The lock being removed by an authorized person (not the original worker). Then: a phone screen showing a call log — attempts to reach the absent employee. Documentation.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Subject-to-subject. The physical act (lock removal) alongside the documentation (phone calls, records). McCloud: the juxtaposition says "these two things happen together — the removal and the notification are inseparable." The image of the phone is mundane, but it represents the protection that extends beyond the physical lock. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Imagine the alternative. A worker comes in for their next shift. They approach the machine they were working on yesterday. They reach in, confident their lock is protecting them.

But the lock was cut eight hours ago. The machine is live.

*[GUTTER: The learner imagines the worker reaching into a live machine, believing they're protected. The implication gutter. Never shown.]*

*[Beat.]*

That's why the notification requirement exists. The lock removal isn't complete until the person who placed it knows. Their safety depends on knowing the state of the machine before they go near it.

## WHO DOES WHAT

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
One more piece. The roles.

The OSHA standard defines four types of employees in a lockout/tagout program. This matters because each role carries different responsibilities, different training requirements, and different obligations.

*[VISUAL: Four roles — Authorized, Affected, Qualified, Other — arranged not as a hierarchy but as concentric circles around the equipment. Authorized closest. Other furthest.]*

<!-- Panel-logic reasoning: Single VISUAL, diagram style. Not a panel sequence. The concentric arrangement teaches the relationship between roles and the equipment better than a list would. McCloud would call this a non-temporal image — it shows a structure, not a narrative. The spatial relationship IS the information. -->

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
**Authorized employees** perform the lockout. They're trained on every energy source, every isolation point, every procedure for the machines they service. They apply the locks. They do the work. They verify the isolation.

**Affected employees** don't perform lockout, but their work is affected by it. An operator whose production line is shut down for maintenance is an affected employee. Their responsibility is clear: never attempt to restart or re-energize a machine that's locked out. Ever. For any reason.

**Qualified employees** work on or near exposed energized parts. They have specialized training in electrical procedures, voltage identification, and clearance distances. They may work alongside authorized employees during lockout.

**Other employees** work in areas where lockout might occur. They receive basic awareness training: lockout exists, locked-out equipment is off-limits, attempting to start a locked machine can kill the person working on it.

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Note that an affected employee can become an authorized employee when their duties change to include servicing equipment. The roles aren't castes. They're responsibilities tied to the work you're currently doing.

The question is always: what are you doing right now, and what do you need to know to do it safely?

## THE PAPERWORK

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Let's talk about the forms.

*[Beat.]*

*[PANEL: A Lockout/Tagout Procedure Form. Filled out. Specific. Energy sources listed. Isolation points identified. Authorized and affected employees named. Dated. Signed.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Most people look at these and see bureaucracy. Another form. Another signature. Another piece of paper between them and the work.

*[Beat.]*

Here's what these forms actually are.

The machine-specific procedure form is the map that tells you every energy source and every isolation point so you don't have to guess.

The training certification is the proof that you were taught this before anyone expected you to do it.

The inspection certification is the evidence that someone watched the procedure being performed correctly and verified it.

The contractor certification is the guarantee that outside personnel know your procedures and you know theirs.

*[Beat.]*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
And the annual review — where an authorized employee who is not involved in the procedure being inspected watches another authorized employee perform it — that's the system checking itself.

Every piece of paper in the lockout/tagout program exists because someone, somewhere, at some point, was hurt or killed by the gap that piece of paper fills.

*[Beat.]*

The paperwork isn't bureaucracy. It's evidence that the system worked and everyone goes home safe.

## MODULE 3 QUIZ

**Question 1:**
During a shift change on a machine that's under lockout, the correct procedure is:
- A) The outgoing crew removes their locks, then the incoming crew applies theirs
- B) The incoming crew applies their locks while the outgoing crew's locks are still in place, then the outgoing crew removes theirs
- C) The outgoing crew leaves their locks for the incoming crew to use
- D) A supervisor holds a master lock during the transition

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 2:**
A worker's lock is on a machine, but they've left the facility without removing it. The correct response is:
- A) Cut the lock immediately — production is delayed
- B) Leave the lock in place indefinitely until the worker returns
- C) Verify the worker is absent, attempt to contact them, remove the lock following emergency procedures, and ensure the worker is notified before returning to work
- D) Have another authorized employee remove the lock since they have the same training

**Correct Answer: C**

**Question 3:**
A machine has room for only one lock on its energy isolation point, but four workers need to service it. The correct approach is:
- A) The team lead places the single lock and holds the key for the group
- B) Workers take turns — one works while the others wait, each applying their lock for their turn
- C) The coordinator locks the machine, places the key in a lockbox, and each worker places their own lock on the lockbox
- D) One lock is sufficient as long as everyone agrees not to remove it

**Correct Answer: C**

**Question 4:**
An operator's production line is shut down because maintenance is performing lockout/tagout on a connected machine. The operator is classified as:
- A) An authorized employee, because they operate the equipment
- B) An affected employee, whose primary responsibility is to never attempt to restart locked-out equipment
- C) A qualified employee, because they understand how the machine works
- D) An other employee, because they aren't involved in the maintenance

**Correct Answer: B**

## PRODUCTION NOTES

### Panel-Logic Decisions

**Cold open (3 panels + gutter):**
- Aspect-to-aspect (shift change atmosphere) → action-to-action (outgoing worker pulls lock) → subject-to-subject (empty disconnect, absent incoming crew)
- Reasoning: The cold open for Module 3 is about TIME — the gap between one crew's protection and the next. Aspect-to-aspect establishes the transitional feeling of shift change. The action-to-action shows the moment protection is removed. The subject-to-subject reveals the vulnerability: empty hasp, no one present. The gutter carries the implication — what could happen in that gap.

**Group lockout — lockbox method (3 panels):**
- Action-to-action × 3. Coordinator locks disconnect → key in lockbox → everyone locks the lockbox.
- Reasoning: This is a procedural build — the learner needs to follow the logic step by step. Action-to-action is McCloud's most efficient narrative transition. Three panels because the lockbox method has three logical steps. By the third panel, the "aha" should have landed.

**Shift change solution (2 panels):**
- Moment-to-moment. New lock on, then old lock off.
- Reasoning: The order is everything. Moment-to-moment forces the learner to see the overlap — both locks present simultaneously. This is the exact inverse of the cold open's error. The pacing makes the solution feel as deliberate as the problem felt careless.

**Emergency lock removal (1 panel + gutter):**
- Subject-to-subject. Lock being removed + phone showing contact attempts.
- Reasoning: The physical act and the documentation happen together. McCloud: juxtaposition as argument. The gutter after this section is the heaviest in the module — the learner imagines a worker returning to a machine they think is locked out.

**Paperwork section:**
- Single panel of the form. Then typography-dominant — no images. The weight comes from the repeated structure: "this form is... this certification is... this review is..." Each sentence reframes paperwork as protection. The absence of images forces the learner to engage with the words.

**Callback (4 panels):**
- Scene-to-scene return → action-to-action (new lock on) → action-to-action (old lock off) → subject-to-subject (worker inside the machine, lock visible in background)
- Reasoning: The callback mirrors the cold open exactly but with correct execution. The learner's brain does the comparison. The final panel — worker inside the machine with the lock visible — is the emotional resolution of the entire course. Safe. Protected. Because the simple procedure was followed.

### Gutter Inventory
1. **Cold open — empty disconnect during gap:** Learner imagines someone starting the machine during the four-minute window
2. **Shift change solution — overlap:** Learner contrasts the seamless overlap with the cold open's gap
3. **Emergency removal — worker returns to "locked" machine:** Learner imagines reaching into a machine they think is safe. The heaviest implication gutter in the course.
4. **Callback — correct procedure:** Relief. Resolution. The system works when it's followed.

### Abstraction Map
- Cold open: Medium detail. The light transition (afternoon → evening) is the most important environmental detail.
- Group lockout: High detail on the hasp and locks. The locks are the teaching element.
- Lockbox method: Medium-high detail. The lockbox itself should be recognizable as a real object.
- Shift change panels: High detail on the hasp. Moment-to-moment demands clarity on what's changing between frames.
- Emergency removal: Medium detail. The phone/documentation adds a mundane, bureaucratic texture that contrasts with the life-or-death stakes.
- Paperwork: Typography-dominant. The form gets one detailed panel. Everything after is voice.
- Callback: Medium detail matching cold open. Consistency signals "same place, different outcome."

### Tone Calibration
- Cold open: Quiet dread. The gap. Not dramatic — just the absence of protection during a transition that happens every day.
- Group lockout: Clear, logical. The lockbox explanation should feel satisfying — an elegant solution to a real constraint.
- Shift change: Precise. The solution is simple and the explanation should be equally clean.
- Emergency removal: Heavy. This is the scenario that should make people uncomfortable. The gutter does the work.
- Roles: Informational. Brisk. This section needs to be clear without being tedious.
- Paperwork: The emotional pivot of the module. The reframing — "paperwork is evidence that the system worked and everyone goes home safe" — earns its weight from everything that came before. This is the quiet thesis.
- Callback: Warm. Resolved. The same scene, done right. Course-closing energy.

### Course-Level Notes
- The three cold opens form their own narrative: Module 1 (the unlocked disconnect), Module 2 (the sign instead of a lock), Module 3 (the shift change gap). Each one is a different failure mode. Together they show the range of ways the simple procedure can fail when it's not followed.
- The closing line of every module is the same: "Everyone goes home safe." This repetition is intentional — it's the callback that spans the course. By Module 3, the learner has earned the full weight of that phrase.
- The "two minutes and a padlock" motif appears in all three modules. The barrier is small. The protection is absolute. The gap between doing it and not doing it is measured in minutes and ounces.
